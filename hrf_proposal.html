The Problem

Bitcoin UX
Challenges in Africa

The protocol works. The interfaces don't. Here's what our research found.

89%

First-Transaction Failure

Of first-time African Bitcoin users fail to complete their first transaction without abandoning or requiring intervention. The failure is not user failure. It is design failure.

73%

Seed Phrase Failure

Cannot complete seed phrase backup — the foundational act of self-custody. No wallet we tested provided a low-literacy alternative to 12-word phrase management.

91%

Ethiopia Abandonment

Of Ethiopian participants abandoned entirely, driven by the complete absence of an Amharic interface. The highest failure rate of any country in our study.

0

USSD Wallets Designed
with African Data

Zero wallets in our study provided USSD access, offline transaction capability, or low-literacy alternatives — for a population of 800 million people.

No organisation is conducting original, open, field-based Bitcoin UX research with African users across multiple countries, multiple wallet categories, and multiple access contexts. This initiative fills that gap — and builds the human infrastructure to sustain that work permanently.

Full session documentation, observation logs, and methodology available for funder review on request. See Annex A for founder background and the evidence base for these figures.
Theory of Change

Sequential,
Evidence-Gated

Each phase is a prerequisite for the next. Nothing is published before it is verified; nothing is taught before it is built from evidence.

01
Field Research
Moderated sessions with real users in 5 countries reveal where and why Bitcoin fails them
02
Published Results
Open country reports and peer-reviewed corpus create a citable, credible evidence base
03
Pattern Library
Findings distilled into reusable USSD and low-literacy design patterns wallet teams can implement
04
Training & Cohorts
A curriculum built from patterns enables local designers to train, test, and improve tools independently
05
Institution
Country anchors sustain research and design improvement after HRF funding ends

Key Assumptions

🔬Research must precede patterns. Design standards without field evidence are guesswork. The pattern library is not built until the first two country reports are published and peer-reviewed.
📖Patterns must precede curriculum. Trainers cannot teach what has not been validated. The training manual is not written until the pattern library is reviewed and stable.
🎓Curriculum must precede cohorts. Running designer training with unvalidated materials wastes participants' time and risks replicating existing failures.
🌍Local ownership is necessary. Externally-produced research consistently fails to produce lasting change in the African tech ecosystem. Country anchors are recruited from within each research cohort.
The Vision

Three things that
don't exist yet.

By 2031, this initiative will have built infrastructure the Bitcoin ecosystem does not currently have anywhere in the world.

01

Verified Open Research Corpus

The deepest field research dataset on Bitcoin usability across African markets — five countries, six wallet categories, multiple languages. Published openly. Continuously updated.

02

USSD & Resilience Design Standard

Tested, open design patterns for Bitcoin over USSD, low-literacy users, offline environments, and surveillance contexts — derived from real sessions, not design studio assumptions.

03

Permanent Local Design Community

Trained Bitcoin UX designers and researchers in all five countries — recruited through cohorts, trained on the methodology, contracted as country anchors. When HRF exits, the institution does not.

Programme of Work

Five phases.
Sequenced by evidence.

Each phase produces the inputs for the next. The budget and milestone schedule follow the same structure.

01
Field Research
Years 1–3 · 5 countries · 400–500 sessions

Phase 1 is the evidence base for everything that follows. No design patterns, no curriculum, no training is produced until there is a published, peer-reviewed country report to draw from. Kenya and Nigeria run first (Year 1); Ghana and South Africa follow (Year 2); Ethiopia completes the corpus (Year 3).

Six Wallet Categories

Primary · Phase 1
Self-Custody Smartphone
Phoenix, Muun, Green, Blue Wallet, Breez. Where most first-time users arrive and most fail.
Critical Gap
USSD & Feature Phone
Machankura + SMS-based services. Highest need. Zero existing user research.
Entry Point
Custodial & Semi-Custodial
Custody comprehension task battery. Actual entry point for most African users.
Remittance Focus
Lightning-Native
Fee structure, channels, remittance flows. Makes Bitcoin viable for small payments.

Exploratory. Activist and journalist threat model sessions. Adapted session design for high-risk participants.
Local Innovation

Identical methodology, equal publication prominence. Local innovation documented with full rigour.
Gates to Phase 2
  • Country research reports — one per country, published openly, with peer reviewer named
  • Full session archive — observation logs, consent forms, task records; available for funder audit
  • AI methodology documentation — what AI did and did not do, how transcripts were processed
02
Publication & Open Corpus
Rolling from Year 2 · Triggered by each country report

Publication is its own phase with its own quality gate. Each country report is peer reviewed before publication. The open corpus is built incrementally as reports are published. The corpus is not declared complete until all five country reports are live and the longitudinal cross-country analysis is published.

Gates to Phase 3
  • Five country reports published openly — each reviewed by an independent researcher with in-country institutional affiliation
  • Open multilingual corpus — full dataset under open licence, continuously updated
  • Cross-country synthesis — patterns that hold across all five markets
  • Wallet failure rate public dashboard — retestable benchmarks per wallet per task, updated annually
Phase 3 does not begin until the Kenya and Nigeria reports are published and at least two additional country reports are in peer review. The pattern library is built from evidence, not ahead of it.
03
Pattern Library & Training Manual
Year 3 · Built from Phase 2 findings

The pattern library and training manual are derivative products — they codify and communicate what the research found. They are produced from the research, not during it. This separation ensures the curriculum teaches what was actually observed, not what was assumed.

Pattern Library

Documented, tested, open design patterns for Bitcoin over USSD, low-literacy onboarding, offline transaction flows, and surveillance-resistant interfaces. Each pattern cites the specific sessions it addresses. Reviewed by at least one wallet team before publication.

Training Manual

A structured curriculum for Bitcoin UX designers in the African context, built directly from the pattern library. Covers: reading the research corpus; applying USSD patterns; running moderated sessions; documenting findings to the initiative's standard. Written for practitioners, not academics.

Gates to Phase 4
  • Bitcoin wallet UX pattern library — published openly; each pattern cites supporting session evidence
  • USSD design standards document and low-literacy design framework — standalone references
  • Training manual v1 — peer reviewed by local designers in Kenya and Nigeria before finalisation
Phase 4 cohorts do not begin until the training manual has passed peer review. Running cohorts with an unreviewed curriculum is a quality risk this sequencing eliminates.
04
Designer Cohorts
Years 3–4 · 15–20 designers per country · 5 cohorts total

Cohorts are delivered country-by-country using the peer-reviewed training manual. Kenya and Nigeria run first (late Year 3), then Ghana and South Africa (Year 4), then Ethiopia (Year 5). Each cohort produces a pool of trained designers from which country anchor candidates are identified.

  • 15–20 designers per country, recruited through existing Bitcoin and tech communities in each market
  • Training covers the full manual: research reading, pattern application, session facilitation, findings documentation
  • Each cohort produces at least one documented usability test conducted by participants — not simulated
  • Completion requires a signed-off session archive to the initiative's standard, not just attendance
05
Institution & Sustainability
Year 5 · HRF exit · 5 country anchors active

Phase 5 is the transition from a grant-funded initiative to a self-sustaining institution. By Year 5, the pattern library is complete, all cohorts are delivered, and five country anchors are operating with contracts transitioning to local funding.

  • Pattern library final edition published — comprehensive, translated, formally released under open licence
  • All 5 country anchors contracted part-time, with sustainability plans for post-HRF anchor funding
  • Sustainability model active — UX support programme, Spiral co-funding, training programme fees
  • HRF exit report — full documentation of the institution, its finances, and its independence
HRF is not funding a project. It is seeding an institution that will exist when the funding ends.

Research Integrity

Accountable
by design.

Sessions Are Verifiable

Every session produces: signed consent form, recording (audio minimum), structured observation log within 24 hours, and task completion record. Available for funder audit on request.

Coordinators Contractually Accountable

Each coordinator has specific deliverables — session count, recruitment criteria, log standards, timeline. Payments are milestone-based, not time-based. Sessions sampled against logs each quarter.

Independent Peer Review Before Publication

Each country report reviewed by at least one qualified external researcher with institutional affiliation in that country. Their name and affiliation appear on the published report.

No Wallet Pays for Inclusion or Exclusion

Explicit, public, non-negotiable. The initiative accepts no funding from wallet developers, exchanges, or payment processors. Selection criteria published: see Annex C.

Participant Safety

Not a bureaucratic
concern.

Running sessions about Bitcoin usage with Ethiopian participants in the current political environment carries real safety risks. Every protocol below exists because the risk is real.

  • Country-specific risk assessments completed before sessions begin in each new country
  • Participant safety protocols reviewed by a researcher with conflict and surveillance context experience
  • Consent framework explicitly addresses digital security — recordings stored with end-to-end encryption, identities protected against state-level actors
  • Local coordinators trained in operational security relevant to their country context before handling any participant data
  • AI-assisted analysis reduces the minimum sessions required in high-risk countries, directly reducing participant exposure
  • No session data leaves the country of origin without explicit consent covering cross-border data transfer
Full data protection and privacy framework: see Annex G.

Founder Dependency Risk

The initiative's primary structural risk is founder dependency. Mitigated through: (1) full methodology documentation published by Month 6; (2) advisory board with independent authority over research integrity; (3) country coordinators trained to run sessions independently from Month 3; (4) all IP held by the legal entity, not personally.

Continuity Plan

In the event the founder cannot continue: all IP, data, and tooling is held by the legal entity; the advisory board has independent authority; a designated deputy coordinator can maintain operations for up to 6 months from the contingency reserve; full methodology is documented and published by Month 6.
