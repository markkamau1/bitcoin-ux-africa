Executive Summary

Bitcoin is the most powerful tool for financial freedom ever built. Africans cannot use it. That is a design failure, and it is solvable.

Six hundred million people across Sub-Saharan Africa live under currency systems that fail them daily. Inflation devours savings overnight. Remittances lose 8% to corridors that move at the speed of bureaucracy. Formal bank accounts require documentation that millions will never possess. Bitcoin exists as a real alternative. But the wallets built to carry it were designed in San Francisco and Berlin, for English speakers with stable broadband and smartphones that cost three months' wages. The result is drop-off rates above 70% on the most basic transaction. This is not a technology problem. It is a design problem, and nobody has systematically studied it.

This initiative exists to do that work, rigorously, openly, and permanently. Phase 0 (340+ moderated sessions, 2023–2025, self-funded) proved the methodology and revealed the scale of the failure. This proposal funds the next five years: a five-country research programme that produces the world's first open usability corpus for Bitcoin in Africa, derives design patterns from that evidence, trains local designers to apply them, and exits leaving an institution with the capacity to continue the work independently.

The urgency is concrete. Most Bitcoin wallets are built for fast broadband, high-end smartphones, and users who have never had to think about data costs. In Africa, the majority of users are on 2G connections, low-memory Android devices, or USSD feature phones with no app capability at all. Every year wallet teams ship to these markets without a single session of research conducted on those devices, in those conditions, with those users. Every year the gap between what wallets assume and what African users actually have grows wider. This proposal funds the evidence base that closes that gap, and it funds the people who will keep closing it long after HRF has exited.

The Problem

The protocol works.
The interfaces don't.

What 340 moderated sessions with African Bitcoin users actually found.

89%

First-Transaction Failure

Of first-time African Bitcoin users fail to complete their first transaction without abandoning or requiring intervention. The failure is not user failure. It is design failure.

73%

Seed Phrase Failure

Cannot complete seed phrase backup, the foundational act of self-custody. No wallet tested provided a low-literacy alternative to 12-word phrase management.

91%

Ethiopia Abandonment

Of Ethiopian participants abandoned entirely, driven by the complete absence of an Amharic interface. The highest failure rate of any country in our study.

0

USSD Wallets Designed with African Data

Zero wallets provided USSD access, offline transaction capability, or low-literacy alternatives, for a population of 800 million people without smartphones.

No organisation is conducting original, open, field-based Bitcoin UX research with African users across multiple countries, multiple wallet categories, and multiple access contexts. This initiative fills that gap, and builds the human infrastructure to sustain that work permanently.

Key Assumptions

🔬Research must precede patterns. Design standards without field evidence are guesswork. The pattern library is not built until the first two country reports are published.
📖Patterns must precede curriculum. Trainers cannot teach what has not been validated. The training manual is not written until the pattern library is reviewed and stable.
🏫️Curriculum must precede cohorts. Running designer training with unvalidated materials wastes participants' time and risks replicating the existing failures this initiative exists to fix.
🌎Local ownership is necessary. Externally-produced research consistently fails to produce lasting change in the African tech ecosystem. Country anchors are recruited from within each research cohort.

Participant Safety

Protecting
research participants.

Research in high-risk environments requires protocols designed specifically for those environments. One-size-fits-all consent forms and data handling are not acceptable here.

Standard Protocol (All Countries)
  • Informed consent in participant's preferred language before any session begins
  • Right to withdraw at any point, with all data destroyed on request
  • No government-accessible cloud storage in any country
  • Session recordings held for 3 years maximum, deleted on schedule
  • No names or identifying information in published findings
Ethiopia-Specific Protocol
  • Safety assessment conducted by advisory board before sessions commence
  • AI pre-screening reduces required session count, limiting participant exposure
  • No data held in-country beyond 90 days
  • Coordinator has authority to pause or cancel at any point without justification to the founder
  • Participants using pseudonyms throughout; no real names in any record

Participant safety sits above research completeness. If safety conditions in Ethiopia deteriorate, research pauses. The advisory board holds explicit authority to suspend activity in any country at any time. This is not a risk mitigation measure. It is a commitment.

Detailed data protection policy, breach notification procedure, and participant rights documentation: see Annex G.

Annex E
Research Methodology

Each country study uses a standardised moderated usability methodology developed and refined during Phase 0. Sessions are 60–90 minutes, conducted in the participant's preferred language, with a structured task battery covering key Bitcoin actions.

ComponentStandardRationale
Session formatModerated think-aloud, 60–90 minutesCaptures failure moments in real time, not retrospective recall
Task battery8–12 standardised tasks per session, wallet-category specificCross-country comparability; failure rates are meaningful only if tasks are identical
LanguageParticipant's preferred language; professional translation of all protocolsLanguage barriers suppress real failure modes; English-only sessions miss the most important findings
RecruitmentNon-expert users with basic mobile money familiarity; no prior Bitcoin experience requiredExpert users mask the design failures that affect the majority
Session archiveAudio recording, observation log within 24 hours, task completion record, signed consentVerifiability; every finding must be traceable to a session
Peer reviewEach country report reviewed by local designers before publicationEnsures findings are grounded in local context
